# 🎬 Can You Judge a Movie's Success by Its Poster?

**DS 4002 Case Study — Movie Poster Visual Analytics**

---

You've just been hired as a **Visual Marketing Analyst** at a major film studio. Your boss walks in with a bold claim: *"I can tell how well a movie will do at the box office just by looking at its poster."* She believes that certain visual patterns in movie posters — vibrant color palettes, specific color grading trends like the famous "orange and teal" look, and the number of faces on the poster — are reliable predictors of a film's worldwide theatrical revenue.

**Your mission:** Use data science to put this claim to the test.

You have access to a dataset of over **1,000 films** with worldwide box office figures. For each film, you will download its official poster and extract quantitative visual features: the dominant color palette, average brightness, color saturation, the prevalence of "Hollywood-style" orange-and-teal color grading, and the density of human faces. You will then apply statistical modeling techniques — including correlation analysis, multiple linear regression, and a machine learning model — to determine whether any of these visual characteristics are statistically significant predictors of global revenue.

**The stakes are real.** Studios spend millions on marketing, and the poster is often the first thing a potential audience member sees. If visual features genuinely predict revenue, it could reshape how marketing teams design campaigns. If they don't, it tells us something equally important about what *actually* drives commercial success in the film industry.

### Your Deliverable

Produce a **short analytical report** (3–5 pages) with supporting visualizations and statistical results that answers the research question: *Do the visual characteristics of a movie poster predict its worldwide box office performance?* Your analysis should include exploratory data analysis, at least one inferential statistical model, and a predictive evaluation. All code and data should be reproducible.

Details on the specific requirements, data sources, and evaluation criteria are provided in the **Rubric**.
